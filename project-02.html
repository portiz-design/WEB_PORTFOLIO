CLOSE ENOUGH

Multi-generational Housing

Architect: Pedro Ortiz
Project Category: Academic, M.Arch
Program: Multi-Generational Housing
Users: Grandparents · Parents · Children
Institution: Rhode Island School of Design, Providence, RI
Software: AutoCAD, V-Ray, Rhino 8, Photoshop
Site Category: Suburban
Structure Composition: Reinforced concrete, brick, wood, glass
Façade: White brick

Concept

Architecture can shape the way families live together. Designed for three generations, this house carefully negotiates proximity and privacy, creating a home where collective life and individual independence coexist in balance.

As housing costs continue to rise, multi-generational living is re-emerging as both an economic strategy and a way for families to support one another. Designed to fit within a compact 50 × 50 ft suburban lot, the house is organized into two primary volumes: a two-level block containing shared living spaces and a second block dedicated to private areas, composed of a two-level and a three-level wing.

Courtyard House

All spaces are arranged around a central courtyard inspired by the Spanish patio, bringing natural light and fresh air into the house while creating a shared outdoor space that connects the family through a gradual transition from private to social life.

The courtyard becomes the heart of the house, a space where light, air, and family life converge.

Ground Level — Arrival

Entry occurs through a foyer that acts as a transitional space between the social block, the private wing, and the central courtyard. Fully glazed along its perimeter, the courtyard visually expands the interior and becomes the spatial element that ties the entire house together.

Private Wing

The ground level of the private wing is designed primarily for the elderly members of the family. Two suite bedrooms—one for the grandparents and one for guests or caregivers—are separated by a secondary courtyard connected to the central patio.

This smaller courtyard improves natural ventilation and daylight while extending views beyond the house. Locating these rooms at ground level allows elderly residents to access the main social spaces without obstacles while remaining visually connected to daily family life.

Social Block

The social block unfolds sequentially along the ground level, beginning with the living room and continuing through the dining area and open kitchen. A tea room completes the sequence and reconnects the social spaces with the private wing.

A central staircase leads to a family terrace above, where play, leisure, and outdoor gatherings extend the social life of the house.

Upper Levels

Two independent staircases located beside the ground-level suites lead to the upper levels of the private wing. One stair leads to the children’s bedroom, while the other connects to an intermediate level containing the parents’ studio or home office.

The parents’ suite occupies the third level, completing the vertical organization of the private block. Careful placement of windows ensures that none of the private rooms face directly into one another, maintaining privacy while allowing generous natural light and ventilation.

Courtyard System

Two interconnected courtyards structure the environmental and spatial organization of the house. The central courtyard acts as the main social heart, bringing light, air, and visual continuity to the surrounding spaces.

The secondary courtyard, located between the ground-level suites, improves cross-ventilation and extends views toward the exterior.

Together, these patios create a system that enhances environmental comfort while reinforcing the spatial relationship between private and collective areas of the house.
